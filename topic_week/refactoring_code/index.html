Nous 2021-12-14 02:51
I will say it is not uncommon to not realise you need to refactor something until you have started working on it. And I find when you get into that situation, stash your changes and go do the refactoring work you need first. Commit and push that (ideally also get it merged into mainline) then continue with your feature work. But not enough people do this. Which results in some horrific PRs to review if the refactor/change was more then a few lines of code. Theses types of PRs tend to get rubber stamped approvals from reviewers and much more likely to introduce regressions. Marcus H 2021-12-14 09:16
One trick I use is to label task/project/module/code as poc/staging/prod so when something is named as boundary-poc (hashicorp boundary proof-of-concept) - everyone knows it can not run in prod and needs to be re-written (refactored) at least once (edited)
Sometimes when the module is extra complicated or extra novel I add iterations for said label eg kubernetes-staging-4 (eg 4th version of kubernetes-staging pipeline)
As you can already notice - using stages (dev/staging/prod) is one way to introduce refactoring in a subtle and self-explanatory way
added benefit for "kubernetes-staging-4" naming scheme is that sometimes deleting the cluster leaves traces to AWS environment, and you do not know it leaves traces until months later
